History of the Christian Faith and the Development of the Christian Church

A comprehensive survey tracing the journey from Eden to the final crisis, highlighting worship, growth, apostasy, and restoration.

The history of the Christian faith is a vast record of the struggle between divine truth and human error. It is a story that begins long before the church had a formal name or a recognized building. To understand the origins of this faith, one must look back to the very beginning of human history, where the foundation of worship was first laid by God Himself in the Garden of Eden. The narrative of the Christian church is not merely a list of dates and names; it is a description of how God has sought to preserve a knowledge of His character in a world that has often been led astray by deception.

The Ancient Foundation of Worship and the Sacrificial System

Before the Christian church was established in its modern form, the people of God followed a system of worship that focused on animal sacrifices. This was not a human invention but a divine appointment intended to teach humanity about the cost of sin and the promise of a future Savior. After the fall of man, the first promise of a Redeemer was given, and the sacrificial system was instituted as a way for humans to express faith in that promise.

The Sanctuary Service in Israel

When the nation of Israel was established, God gave specific instructions to Moses on Mount Sinai for the building of a sanctuary. This sanctuary was the center of Jewish national and religious life. The sacrificial system performed there was designed to burn into the hearts of the people an awareness of their own sin and the necessity of atonement. Through the unceasing sacrifice of animals, the Israelites were taught that sin leads to death and that only an innocent life could serve as a substitute.

The Torah, specifically the book of Leviticus, outlines five main types of offerings that formed the core of this worship. The burnt offering was perhaps the most prominent. It required a male animal without blemish—a bull, lamb, or goat, depending on the offeror's wealth. The worshiper would lay his hand on the animal's head, symbolically transferring his sin to the innocent victim, before the animal was killed. This sacrifice was entirely consumed by fire on the altar, representing a total dedication of one's life to God and a request for a renewed relationship with Him.

In addition to the burnt offering, there were grain offerings made of fine flour, oil, and frankincense, which were the only sacrifices that did not involve blood. These represented an acknowledgment of God’s provision. Peace offerings were voluntary sacrifices given as a way to thank God for His generosity or to fulfill a vow. Sin offerings and trespass offerings were required for specific sins committed unintentionally or for violations of God’s laws. For each of these, specific procedures were followed, often involving the sprinkling of blood on the altar or the burning of fat.

The Symbolism of Blood and Atonement

The central element of this ancient worship was the use of blood. In Jewish culture, blood was perceived as the life force of the creature. Leviticus 17:11 explains that "the life of the flesh is in the blood," and it was given upon the altar to make atonement for the soul. This belief established that the potency of a sacrifice was proportional to the value of the life offered. While animal blood was effective for a temporary, ceremonial cleansing, it was understood to have finite power. This necessitated the constant repetition of sacrifices year after year.

The most solemn day in the Jewish calendar was Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement. On this day, the high priest performed elaborate rituals to cleanse the entire community of their sins and to purify the sanctuary itself. The blood of animals was symbolically used to remove the record of sin from the presence of God. However, this entire system was a "shadow of things to come." It was a temporary arrangement meant to point the worshipers toward the "Lamb of God," whose death would provide a once-for-all atonement for the sins of the world.

Prayer and Worship Before the Formal Church

Before the era of formal church buildings, people prayed and worshiped in a variety of settings. The patriarchs like Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob built altars of stone wherever they traveled. Their worship was personal and direct, characterized by simple faith in God’s promises. Once the tabernacle and later the temple in Jerusalem were built, worship became more institutionalized, but the core remained the same: a recognition of human dependence on a holy God. Prayer was often viewed as an "offering of the lips," and in some Jewish communities, such as those at Qumran, the prayer of the righteous was seen as a spiritual substitute for the physical sacrifices when access to the temple was restricted.

Jesus Christ as the Foundation of the Christian Church

The Christian faith does not stand as a separate entity from the history of Israel; rather, it is the fulfillment of it. Jesus Christ is the central figure who bridged the gap between the old sacrificial system and the new spiritual reality of the church. He was a Jew who lived under the law, and His ministry was the culmination of all the promises made to the Hebrew patriarchs.

The Role of Jesus in Founding the Church

Jesus began His ministry by announcing that the kingdom of God was at hand. He called twelve disciples to be His closest followers, a number that symbolically represented the twelve tribes of Israel, indicating that He was ushering in a new covenant for God’s people. The foundation of the church was laid when the apostle Peter confessed that Jesus was "the Christ, the Son of the living God." In response, Jesus declared that upon this "rock"—the truth of His divinity—He would build His church.

Jesus played the role of the Prophet, Priest, and spiritual King. He was the "Jehovah of the ancient church" who had spoken through the prophets and was now personally present to demonstrate the character of God. His teachings emphasized a shift from outward rituals to inward transformation. He taught that God is a Spirit and must be worshiped in spirit and in truth. By doing so, He was preparing His followers for a time when the physical temple in Jerusalem would no longer be the center of worship.

The Great Sacrifice and the End of the Types

The death of Jesus on the cross was the moment when the ancient sacrificial system met its fulfillment and its end. As the "Lamb of God," His sacrifice was infinite in power and eternal in duration. Unlike the animal sacrifices that had to be repeated, Jesus' death was a once-for-all atonement for sin. When He died, the veil of the temple was torn in two, signifying that the way to the presence of God was now open to everyone through Him, and the need for earthly priests and animal sacrifices had ceased.

The book of Hebrews provides a detailed theological explanation for this shift. It argues that Jesus entered the heavenly temple with His own blood, achieving eternal redemption for humanity. This transition changed the nature of worship forever. The church was no longer bound to a specific building or a specific nation; it was a global community of believers connected to their High Priest in heaven.

The Birth and Formation of the Early Church

What resulted after the death and resurrection of Jesus was a transformation of His followers. Initially, the disciples were scattered and filled with fear. However, the reality of the resurrection gave them a new direction and a courage that the powers of the world could not break.

The Event of Pentecost

The symbolic birth of the Christian church is marked by the event of Pentecost, which occurred fifty days after the resurrection. As the believers were gathered in Jerusalem, the Holy Spirit was poured out upon them. They experienced the sound of a rushing wind and saw tongues of fire, and they were given the ability to speak in the various languages of the people gathered in the city for the festival.

The apostle Peter, who had previously denied Jesus, stood up and preached a powerful sermon. He explained that the outpouring of the Spirit was the fulfillment of the prophecy of Joel and that Jesus, whom the people had crucified, was indeed the Messiah. His words led to the baptism of about 3,000 people on that single day. This event transformed the small band of followers into a missional community empowered to carry the message of Jesus from Jerusalem to the ends of the earth.

How the Early Church Was Formed

The early church was characterized by simplicity and a strong sense of community. The believers "continued steadfastly in the apostles' doctrine and fellowship, and in breaking of bread, and in prayers". They lived with a shared commitment to the teachings of Jesus, and their love for one another became a powerful witness to the world. They met in house churches and shared their resources so that no one among them was in need.

The mission of the church was driven by the expectation of the imminent Second Coming of Christ. They believed their task was to urgently preach the gospel to prepare the world for His kingdom. As the movement grew, it became necessary to appoint leaders, such as deacons and elders, to manage the practical needs of the community and to preserve the theological integrity of the message. The church remained flexible and missional, often expanding into new territories through the work of traveling evangelists.

Crossing Cultural and Ethnic Boundaries

One of the most significant moments in the formation of the church was the decision to open the doors to Gentiles without requiring them to become Jews first. This decision, confirmed at the Jerusalem Council in Acts 15, allowed the gospel to spread rapidly across the Roman Empire. The church began to cross racial, class, and traditional barriers, proving that the message of Jesus was intended for all of humanity.

The apostle Paul became the leading figure in this expansion, establishing churches in Asia Minor, Greece, and Rome.

The Acceptance and Rejection of the Christians

As the church expanded, it encountered a wide range of responses. The gospel found a springboard in Jewish synagogues, but it also met intense opposition from those who saw it as a threat to their established traditions.

Who Accepted the Christians?

Many among the common people of the Roman Empire accepted the Christian faith. Slaves, in particular, found the message of equality and divine love to be deeply appealing. In a world where they were often treated as "lifeless tools," Christianity offered them dignity and a hope that transcended their earthly circumstances. Women also played a significant role in the early church, often being the first to accept the message in new cities, such as Lydia in Philippi.

The faith also appealed to people of different cultures because it offered a universal identity. By the end of the second century, there were well-established churches in Gaul (modern France), Latin Africa (Carthage), and Alexandria. Even people from the Germanic tribes and as far away as India began to embrace the faith.

Who Did Not Accept the Christians?

The Jewish religious leaders were among the first to reject the Christian movement, seeing it as a heresy that undermined the law of Moses. This opposition led to the first persecutions of Christians, including the stoning of Stephen and the execution of James.

The Roman Empire also became a major opponent of the church. Initially, the Romans viewed Christians as a Jewish sect, but as the movement grew and distanced itself from Judaism, it became a target of official suspicion. Because Christians refused to participate in the imperial cult or worship the Roman gods, they were declared "rebels against the empire" and "foes of religion". They were falsely accused of causing natural disasters and were mocked for worshiping a crucified man. This unofficial mockery and official persecution were intended to stop the spread of the faith, but they often had the opposite effect.

The Fates of the Apostles and the First Martyrs

The growth of the church was marked by the willingness of its leaders and members to die for their beliefs. The stories of the apostles and the early martyrs provide a powerful record of devotion and sacrifice.

What Happened to All the Disciples?

The original twelve apostles, along with Paul, took the commission to "go and make disciples of all nations" seriously. Their travels took them to the farthest reaches of the known world, and most of them eventually faced martyrdom.

  • Peter: He is traditionally believed to have been crucified in Rome during the reign of Nero. It is said he was crucified upside down because he felt unworthy to die in the same manner as Jesus.
  • Paul: After his extensive missionary labors throughout the Mediterranean, Paul was imprisoned in Rome and eventually beheaded under Nero.
  • Andrew: He traveled to the regions around the Black Sea and into Greece. He was crucified on an X-shaped cross in Achaia.
  • James the Son of Zebedee: He was the first of the twelve to be martyred, executed with a sword by King Herod Agrippa.
  • Thomas: Known for his mission to India, Thomas is said to have established the church there before being killed by a spear.
  • Matthew: He is reported to have ministered in Ethiopia and Parthia before his death.
  • Bartholomew: He served as a missionary in India, Armenia, and Mesopotamia. Tradition says he was martyred by being flayed alive and then beheaded.
  • John: He was the only apostle believed to have died of old age. He was exiled to the island of Patmos, where he wrote the book of Revelation, but he eventually returned to Ephesus.
  • James the Son of Alphaeus: He is often associated with ministry in Egypt or Syria and was martyred, though accounts of the method vary.
  • Simon the Zealot: He is said to have traveled through Africa and even to Britain before being crucified.
  • Philip: He was active in Phrygia and was martyred in the city of Hierapolis.
  • Matthias: Chosen to replace Judas, he is reported to have preached in "the land of the man-eaters" and was eventually martyred.

The First and Following Martyrs

Stephen was the first Christian martyr, and his death sparked a wave of persecution that helped spread the gospel as believers fled Jerusalem. Following him, thousands of Christians died in the Roman arenas. During the first few centuries, there were several waves of systematic persecution. Under Nero, Christians were burned as torches in his gardens or torn by wild beasts for public entertainment. In A.D. 250, Emperor Decius required all citizens to perform pagan sacrifices, leading to a new wave of deaths for those who refused.

Despite these horrors, the church continued to grow. The early Christian writings, such as the stories of Perpetua and Felicitas, show that the martyrs faced death with a peace that amazed their executioners. This strength was seen as evidence of the truth of their message, and many people were converted as a result of seeing the martyrs' courage.

The Corruption of the Church and the Change of the Sabbath

The devil, finding that he could not destroy the church through persecution, turned to a more deceptive strategy: corruption from within. As the fierce persecutions of the first few centuries ceased, the church began to experience temporal prosperity and worldly honor.

The Entrance of Apostasy

During the fourth century, Christianity became a favored religion of the state. The nominal conversion of Emperor Constantine led to a flood of paganism entering the church. Idolaters joined the Christian ranks without a true change of heart, bringing their rituals and superstitions with them. The church laid aside the humble simplicity of Christ for the pomp and pride of pagan priests and rulers. This compromise resulted in the development of "the man of sin" foretold in prophecy—a system that exalted human authority over the authority of God.

The Bible was no longer accepted as the sole standard of faith. Human theories and traditions were substituted for the requirements of God. Images were introduced into churches, and the worship of saints began to take the place of the worship of God. The "mystery of iniquity" worked silently at first, but it eventually gained control of the minds of men and the power of the state.

How the Sabbath Was Changed

One of the most significant marks of this corruption was the change of the Sabbath from the seventh day to the first day of the week. In the first centuries, the true Sabbath had been kept by all Christians in obedience to the fourth commandment. To make Christianity more acceptable to the pagan sun-worshipers, church leaders began to promote Sunday as a festival in honor of the resurrection.

In A.D. 321, Constantine issued a decree making Sunday a public festival throughout the Roman Empire. Initially, it was called the "venerable day of the sun" to appeal to both pagans and Christians. Over time, the church at Rome used its influence to exalt Sunday and suppress the observance of the seventh-day Sabbath. Eventually, those who continued to keep the Bible Sabbath were declared "accursed" by the church councils. This change was not based on the Bible but on human tradition and the desire for worldly power.

The Dark Ages and the Preservation of Truth

The Papal Supremacy and the Suppression of Scripture

For nearly a thousand years, the church entered a period of spiritual darkness known as the Dark Ages. During this time, the papacy held supreme power, and the Bible was largely hidden from the common people.

The bishop of Rome was declared to be the head of the universal church and was given titles belonging only to God. The claim was made that the pope was infallible and had the authority to change God’s laws. To maintain this power, the church prohibited the common people from reading the Bible in their own language. It was kept in Latin, which only the educated could read, and the priests became the only source of spiritual knowledge.

This suppression of the Word of God led to the rise of false doctrines, such as the sale of indulgences, the belief in purgatory, and the idea that salvation could be earned through works and ceremonies. Those who dared to disagree with the church's teachings were often branded as heretics and faced the horrors of the Inquisition.

The Church in the Wilderness: The Waldenses

Even during the Dark Ages, God had a faithful people who refused to bow to the authority of Rome. One of the most notable groups was the Waldenses. They lived in the remote valleys of the Alps and preserved the ancient faith of the apostles. They had the Bible in their own language and taught their children to memorize large portions of it.

The Waldenses were a missionary people. They traveled as merchants and peddlers, secretly sharing the scriptures with those they met. They continued to observe the seventh-day Sabbath and the simple worship of the early church. Despite centuries of crusades and persecutions directed against them, they remained a "light in the darkness," keeping the flame of truth alive until the time of the Reformation.

The Reformation: Bringing the Bible Back to the People

The work of restoring the church to its biblical foundation began with the Reformers. They were men of learning and courage who were willing to risk their lives to bring the Word of God back to the people.

John Wycliffe: The Morning Star of the Reformation

John Wycliffe was a brilliant scholar at Oxford University in the fourteenth century. As he studied the scriptures in their original languages, he discovered the errors of the church and began to speak out against them. He attacked the corruption of the monks and the usurpations of the papacy.

Wycliffe's greatest contribution was the translation of the Bible into English. He believed that the people needed the Bible in their own tongue to protect them from the deceptions of the priests. Despite receiving several papal bulls ordering his arrest, Wycliffe was protected by divine providence and the support of the English people. His work laid the foundation for the Reformation in England and inspired other reformers in Europe, such as John Huss and Jerome of Prague.

Martin Luther and the Discovery of the Bible

Martin Luther was a German monk who was deeply burdened by his sense of sin. While he was a student at the University of Erfurt, he discovered a Latin Bible in the library—a book he had never seen before. This discovery changed his life. He began to study the Word of God with an intense hunger, often robbing himself of sleep and food to continue his research.

Luther's impact on the church was profound. He rediscovered the truth that salvation is a free gift from God through faith in Jesus Christ, not something that can be earned through human efforts or the rituals of the church. In 1517, he posted 95 Theses against the sale of indulgences, an act that sparked the Protestant Reformation. At the Diet of Worms, he made his famous stand, declaring that his conscience was "bound by the Word of God" and that he would not take back anything he had written unless he was convinced by the testimony of Scripture.

Luther's work led to the translation of the Bible into the German language and the establishment of churches based on the principle of Sola Scriptura—the Bible alone as the rule of faith and practice. This period marked a "world-awakening" and the unsealing of the fountain of God’s Word for the masses.

The Rise of Many Denominations and the Great Advent Movement

The Advent Awakening

In the early nineteenth century, a new movement arose that transcended denominational boundaries. This was the Great Advent Awakening. People in various parts of the world began to study the prophecies of Daniel and Revelation, particularly those concerning the second coming of Jesus.

William Miller, a Baptist farmer in New York, became the leading figure in this movement in America. Through his study of the 2,300-day prophecy in Daniel 8:14, he became convinced that Jesus would return to earth around 1843 or 1844. Miller was not seeking to start a new church; his goal was to warn the world and prepare people for the Lord’s coming. Thousands of people from various denominations accepted his message and became known as Millerites or Adventists.

The Disappointment of 1844 and the Birth of Seventh-day Adventism

When Jesus did not return on October 22, 1844, the believers experienced a "Great Disappointment." The movement split into several factions, with many losing their faith. However, a small group returned to their Bibles to understand where they had gone wrong. They discovered that the date was correct, but the event was misunderstood. The "cleansing of the sanctuary" in Daniel 8:14 did not refer to the earth but to a work of judgment and intercession that Jesus began in the most holy place of the heavenly sanctuary.

This group, which included pioneers like Joseph Bates, James White, and Ellen G. White, began to see the importance of the seventh-day Sabbath and the other "pillars" of the faith. They realized that God was calling them to restore the truths that had been lost during the centuries of apostasy. Ellen White's visions provided administrative and spiritual guidance, helping the developing denomination to stay focused on its mission.

Christianity Today and the Final Conflict

The State of the Church Today

Today, the Christian church remains divided into many denominations, but the original controversy between Christ and Satan continues to shape its history. The church was formed by God Himself, but the devil has worked relentlessly to corrupt it through false doctrines and worldly compromise.

Biblical Warnings About Future Persecution

The Bible and the writings of Ellen White provide clear warnings about the challenges that the church will face before the end of time. The book of Daniel speaks of a "time of trouble, such as never was since there was a nation". Jesus also warned His disciples that they would be arrested, persecuted, and killed, and that these events would intensify as His return draws closer.

Final Crisis and the Sabbath

The final conflict will center on the law of God, particularly the Sabbath. Prophecy indicates that the papacy will regain its power and will work with apostate Protestantism to enforce a universal Sunday law—the "mark of the beast". Those who choose to remain faithful to the fourth commandment and the other requirements of God will face a time of intense trial and persecution. However, the Bible promises that God will deliver His people and that those whose names are written in the book of life will be saved.

Timeline of Key Events in Christian History

  • Before the Formal Church: The institution of the sacrificial system and the building of the earthly sanctuary to point toward the Messiah.
  • The Ministry of Jesus (A.D. 27–31): Jesus establishes the foundation of the church, fulfills the ancient types through His death, and is resurrected.
  • The Apostolic Era (A.D. 31–100): The event of Pentecost, the rapid spread of the church to the Gentiles, and the martyrdom of the apostles.
  • The Era of Persecution (A.D. 64–313): Christians face waves of Roman persecution under emperors like Nero, Domitian, and Decius.
  • The Great Apostasy Begins (A.D. 321): Emperor Constantine issues the first Sunday law, marking the compromise between the church and paganism.
  • The Dark Ages (A.D. 538–1798): The period of papal supremacy, the suppression of the Bible, and the preservation of truth by the Waldenses.
  • The Pre-Reformation (14th–15th Centuries): John Wycliffe translates the Bible into English; John Huss and Jerome of Prague follow his lead.
  • The Protestant Reformation (16th Century): Martin Luther discovers the Bible and posts the 95 Theses, leading to a worldwide religious awakening.
  • The Great Advent Movement (1830s–1844): William Miller and others preach the second coming based on the 2,300-day prophecy.
  • The Birth of Seventh-day Adventism (1844–Present): The discovery of the heavenly sanctuary doctrine and the restoration of the seventh-day Sabbath.
  • The Final Crisis (Future): The enforcement of the mark of the beast, the time of trouble, and the glorious return of Jesus Christ.

Conclusion

The history of the Christian faith is a testimony to God's love and His determination to save humanity. Despite the efforts of the devil to destroy and corrupt the church, the light of truth has never been completely extinguished. As the final chapters of this earth's history are written, the same God who led His people through the Red Sea and supported the martyrs in the arena will lead His church to its ultimate and glorious victory at the Second Advent.
