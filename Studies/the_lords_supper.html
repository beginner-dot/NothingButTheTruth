The Meaning and Importance of the Lord's Supper

As Taught by Jesus Christ of Nazareth

The Historical and Typological Foundation of the Passover

The institution of the Lord's Supper was not a sudden or isolated event in the ministry of Jesus Christ. It was a carefully timed transition, occurring at the precise moment when the shadows of the Old Testament were about to meet the reality of the New. For nearly fifteen centuries, the Jewish people had observed the Passover as their primary national and religious festival. This observance was rooted in the miraculous deliverance of the Israelites from Egyptian bondage, an event where the blood of a lamb, applied to the doorposts of their homes, served as a protective sign against the destroying angel. When Jesus gathered His disciples in the upper room, He was preparing to shift the focus of this ancient memorial from a past deliverance from physical slavery to a future and eternal deliverance from the spiritual slavery of sin.

The Passover lamb was the central figure of the old economy. It had to be a specimen without blemish, representing the absolute purity of the coming Messiah. The sacrificial system, spanning four thousand years of human history, had one consistent purpose: to point the eyes of faith toward the "Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world". As Jesus ate the Passover with His disciples, He stood as the fulfillment of every type and ceremony associated with that system. He was the reality to which the blood of millions of sacrificial animals had pointed. By instituting the Lord's Supper in the place of the Passover, Christ declared that the national festival of the Jews was to pass away forever, making room for a service that would be observed by His followers in all lands and through all ages.

The transition from the Passover to the Lord's Supper also involved a change in the physical emblems used to represent God's grace. While the Passover involved the slaying of a physical lamb and the eating of its flesh, the Lord's Supper utilized the simple, everyday elements of bread and the fruit of the vine. These elements were already present on the Passover table, but Jesus invested them with a new and deeper significance. The unleavened bread, which had previously reminded Israel of the haste of their departure from Egypt, became the symbol of Christ's own pure and sinless body, broken for the life of the world. The wine, which had been a part of the festival's celebration, was redefined as the blood of the new covenant, shed for the remission of sins.

This shift was essential because the ceremonial law was a "shadow of things to come," and once the "body" which cast the shadow—Christ Himself—had arrived, the shadows were no longer necessary. The Lord's Supper acts as a bridge between the two great events of redemptive history: the first coming of Christ to die as a sacrifice, and His second coming to reign as King. By connecting these two events through a simple meal, Jesus ensured that His followers would never lose sight of the cost of their salvation or the glory of their ultimate hope.

The Ordinance of Humility and the Heart of Service

Before the actual partaking of the bread and the wine, Jesus introduced a preparatory rite that is often referred to as the "Ordinance of Humility." This act of foot washing, recorded in the Gospel of John, was a direct response to the spiritual condition of the disciples at that moment. Despite having spent three years with the Master, the disciples were still plagued by personal ambition and a competitive spirit. As they entered the upper room, they had been arguing about who among them would be the greatest in Christ's kingdom. This spirit of self-exaltation made them entirely unfit for the deep spiritual communion that Jesus intended to share with them.

In the social context of the time, foot washing was a common act of hospitality, usually performed by the lowest servant in a household to remove the dust from a guest's feet after a journey along the dry, dusty roads of Judea. On this night, however, there was no servant present, and none of the disciples were willing to humble themselves to perform the task for one another. By rising from the table, girding Himself with a towel, and beginning to wash the feet of His followers, Jesus performed a task that shocked the disciples and provided a profound lesson in the nature of true greatness.

The significance of the foot washing is dual: it is both an act of cleansing and a lesson in humility. Jesus told Peter, "If I wash thee not, thou hast no part with me," indicating that this physical act represented a necessary spiritual purification. While the disciples had already been "bathed" in the sense of their initial conversion and commitment to Christ, they still needed the "washing of the feet" to remove the "dust" of pride and selfishness that had accumulated through their recent strife. For the modern believer, this ordinance serves as a preparatory service to the Lord's Supper, providing a dedicated time for self-examination, the confession of sins, and the reconciling of differences.

The importance of this ordinance as a permanent part of Christian worship has been a subject of study among prominent members of the early Advent movement. Uriah Smith and other pioneers argued that since Jesus explicitly commanded His disciples, "Ye also ought to wash one another's feet," and "I have given you an example, that ye should do as I have done to you," the practice carries the weight of a divine command. It was viewed not merely as an Eastern custom of hospitality, but as a "sacred rite" and a "legacy to the Christian church". The ordinance is designed to keep believers humble, separate from the world, and protected from the spirit of backsliding.

Furthermore, the presence of Judas Iscariot at the foot-washing service adds a layer of depth to the meaning of the ordinance. Jesus knew that Judas had already betrayed Him in his heart, yet He did not exclude him from this act of service. By washing the feet of His enemy, Jesus demonstrated the ultimate reaches of divine love and gave the traitor one final, silent call to repentance. This teaches the church that the ordinance is not a time for human judgment or the exclusion of others based on perceived unworthiness. Instead, it is an "open communion" where the grace of Christ is offered to all, and the responsibility for partaking worthily lies with the individual and their own self-examination.

This "Ordinance of Humility" is intended to result in a "holy calm" and a "reconciling of differences" before the bread and wine are touched. It is a time when "hearts are cleansed by Christ's most precious blood" and "self-distrust" leads to a greater reliance on the Savior. By the time the disciples returned to the table after the washing, the spirit of rivalry had been replaced by a spirit of solemnity and love, preparing them for the institution of the bread and the cup.

The Bread of Life: Symbolism and Spiritual Nourishment

When the preparatory service was concluded, Jesus took the bread, gave thanks, broke it, and gave it to His disciples, saying, "Take, eat; this is my body". The meaning of the bread in the Lord's Supper is deeply rooted in the concept of spiritual nourishment and the necessity of internalizing the life of Christ. In His earlier teachings, as recorded in the Gospel of John, Jesus had identified Himself as the "Bread of Life," stating that "he that eateth of this bread shall live for ever". The bread used at the Last Supper was unleavened, a detail of significant theological importance.

Leaven, which causes dough to rise and ferment, is frequently used in the Bible as a symbol of sin, malice, and hypocrisy. By using unleavened bread, Jesus provided a fit emblem for His own sinless life. Nothing that was "corrupted by fermentation," which is essentially a process of death and decay, could represent the "unblemished sacrifice" of the Son of God. The purity of the bread was a testament to the perfection of the life that was being offered for the redemption of the world.

The act of "breaking" the bread was a prophetic reference to the physical suffering that Christ was about to endure on the cross. Just as the grain must be crushed and ground into flour to become bread, and just as the bread must be broken to be distributed, so the body of Christ was to be "broken for you". This brokenness is the source of life for the believer. Participation in the eating of the bread signifies a personal acceptance of Christ's sacrifice and a "vital connection" with Him. It is an acknowledgment that without the life given by Jesus, the soul is in a state of spiritual starvation.

Prominent early members of the faith emphasized that partaking of the bread is not a mere ritual but a "spiritual feeding". To eat the flesh of the Son of Man is to "internalize His words" and to allow His character to be formed within the soul. It is a "union with Christ" that is described through metaphors like the "vine and the branches," where the believer receives spiritual power and life directly from the Savior. This connection is maintained through "living, active faith" and a "daily surrender of the will".

The bread also carries a communal meaning. The Apostle Paul notes that "we being many are one bread, and one body: for we are all partakers of that one bread". In the act of eating together from the same loaf, the believers proclaim their unity in Christ. The bread represents the "body of Christ" in two senses: the physical body that was sacrificed on the cross, and the spiritual body of the church that is nourished by that sacrifice. This unity is not based on social status, race, or human achievement, but on the shared necessity of the Bread of Life for every soul.

The Lord's Supper was designed to be observed "frequently" so that the followers of Christ would never forget their "continual dependence" upon Him. In a world that often encourages self-sufficiency, the act of eating the bread is a humble admission that our spiritual life is entirely a gift from another. It keeps fresh in the memory the "solemn scenes of his betrayal and crucifixion" and ensures that the "redemption of his people" remains the central focus of their existence.

The Cup of the New Covenant: Blood, Life, and Promise

Following the distribution of the bread, Jesus took the cup and said, "This cup is the new covenant in my blood, which is shed for you". The importance of the "blood of the covenant" is a theme that runs throughout the entire biblical narrative. Under the old system, blood was the means of atonement, but "it is not possible that the blood of bulls and of goats should take away sins". The cup of the Lord's Supper represents the "precious blood of Christ," which has the actual power to cleanse the conscience and reconcile humanity to God.

Just as with the bread, the nature of the drink used in the cup is of great symbolic importance. The "fruit of the vine" used by Jesus was unfermented juice. Fermentation is a symbol of sin and death, whereas the pure juice of the grape represents the "unblemished" and life-giving nature of Christ's blood. The "cleansing blood" must be applied to the heart through faith, and the ordinance of the cup serves as a regular reminder of this necessity. It is the "new testament" or "new covenant," a promise of forgiveness and spiritual renewal that is ratified by the death of the Son of God.

The "New Covenant" introduced by Jesus is a fulfillment of the promise made through the prophet Jeremiah, where God declared He would write His law on the hearts of His people and "remember their sin no more". Partaking of the cup is a public and personal testimony of entering into this covenant relationship. It is an act of "communion" (koinonia), which implies a deep "participation" or "sharing" in the life and benefits of Christ's death. By drinking from the cup, the believer declares their reliance on the blood of Jesus for "pardon and cleansing".

The Apostle Paul provides a warning that the "cup of the Lord" cannot be mixed with the "cup of devils". This highlights the exclusive nature of the Christian's commitment to Christ. To partake of the cup is to "proclaim the Lord's death" as the only source of salvation and to renounce all other forms of spiritual allegiance. This proclamation is not just a silent thought but a collective act of the church, an address to both God and the world about the reality of the Gospel. It is a "testimony of Christ's return," as the believers look forward to the day they will drink it "new in the kingdom of God".

The Lord's Supper, through the cup, commemorates a "far greater event than the deliverance of the children of Israel from Egypt". It celebrates the "salvaging of the world" from the power of darkness. This solemn ordinance was intended to result in "mutual confessions" and a "forgiveness of each other's errors," as the participants realize the immense debt of sin that Christ has paid on their behalf. The cup is a "sign of our bonds of peace," reminding the church that they have been purchased at a great cost and are now "one in us," as Christ prayed for His disciples.

Self-Examination and the Discernment of the Lord's Body

One of the most vital teachings concerning the Lord's Supper is the necessity of "self-examination" before partaking. The Apostle Paul wrote to the Corinthian church, "But let a man examine himself, and so let him eat of that bread, and drink of that cup". This instruction was given because some in the early church were partaking in an "unworthy manner," treating the sacred memorial as a common feast and failing to "discern the Lord's body". To partake unworthily is to be "guilty of the body and blood of the Lord," eating and drinking "judgment" to oneself.

The process of self-examination is not meant to be a time of "sorrowing" or a "remembering of sins" in a way that leads to despair. Instead, the "preparatory service"—the ordinance of humility—is the time for the confession of sin and the reconciling of differences. By the time the believer comes to the table, they are meant to "meet with Christ" in the "saving light" of the cross. The examination is a search of the heart to see if one is harborng pride, selfishness, or a spirit of rivalry, like the disciples in the upper room. It is a moment of "self-distrust" that asks, "Lord, is it I?" when faced with the reality of human betrayal.

To "discern the Lord's body" means to recognize the spiritual reality behind the symbols. It is to understand that the bread and wine represent a "great sacrifice" and an "infinite love". It also means to recognize the "body" of the church—to realize that how we treat our brothers and sisters in Christ is directly related to our relationship with Him. If there is division, strife, or a lack of love among the members, they are failing to discern the body, and their participation in the supper becomes a contradiction of the very unity the service is meant to represent.

This spiritual discipline ensures that the ordinance remains a "vital part of Christian worship" and a means of "religious growth".

The Role of Judas and the Message of Divine Mercy

The inclusion of Judas Iscariot in the first Lord's Supper provides one of the most sobering and yet hopeful lessons in the ministry of Jesus. Christ knew "who should betray Him" long before the event took place, yet He continued to include Judas among the twelve, giving him every opportunity to see His divinity and repent of his selfish purposes. In the upper room, Jesus even performed the humble service of washing the traitor's feet, showing that "no appeal that the divine-human heart of Christ could make had been spared".

The presence of Judas at the table highlights the "power of sin and evil" even in the closest circles of religious life. It serves as a warning that one can be in the physical presence of the Savior, partaking of the sacred emblems, and yet remain unchanged in heart if they cling to "stubborn pride". Judas's decision to leave the "sacramental supper" and "go out into the night" to complete his betrayal is a tragic example of the "last call to repentance" being rejected.

However, Christ's treatment of Judas also reveals the "infinite love" and "waves of mercy" that characterize the Godhead. Jesus did not publicly denounce Judas or forcefully remove him; instead, He spoke in a way that gave Judas the "convincing evidence of His divinity" by reading the "secret purpose of the traitor's heart". This was a final attempt to "subdue" the heart of the false disciple through love rather than condemnation.

The early pioneers of the faith used this example to argue for an "open communion". Because Jesus washed the feet of Judas and allowed him to be present for the bread and wine, it was understood that the church should not take upon itself the role of judging the motives of those who present themselves at the Lord's table. While the "line of division" is drawn by the requirements of baptism and a consistent Christian life, the ultimate responsibility for "eating and drinking unworthily" rests with the individual and their relationship with God.

In pronouncing the "woe upon Judas," Christ also had a "purpose of mercy toward His disciples". He gave them "crowning evidence of His Messiahship" so that when the betrayal occurred, they would not be overwhelmed but would "believe that I AM". The story of Judas in the context of the Lord's Supper is a permanent reminder that the "salvation of men depends upon a continual application" of the blood of Christ, and that even those who have been "chosen" must maintain a "living, active faith" to remain in union with Him.

Unity, Community, and the Body of Christ

The Lord's Supper is the primary "basis of the church's unity" as the "body of Christ". In a world characterized by "noisy, restless strife" and social division, the communion service offers a "holy calm" and a "golden hour" where the people of God are brought into "one bread, and one body". This unity is not a mere organizational agreement but a "spiritual, vital, heart, mystical, indissoluble, moral union" that connects believers to Christ and to one another.

By partaking of the "one loaf" and the "one cup," the church testifies to the truth that "division is dead" in Christ. The Lord's Supper is an "act of rebellion against the ways of the world," which prioritizes hierarchy and exclusion. At the Lord's table, "Sierra and Senegal, Armenia and America, China and Chad" are all brought together in "purposeful unity". The "blood of Jesus was spilled to welcome us into union with Him and each other," and to disregard this unity is to disregard the "great sacrifice" of the cross.

The "Ordinance of Humility" plays a critical role in fostering this community. It is designed to "result in mutual confessions" and to "increase feelings of forbearance, forgiveness of each other's errors, and true love". It is the "ideal preparation" for the Lord's Supper because it levels the participants, reminding them that they are all "dependent on God and upon one another". A meal, in the biblical sense, is a "teacher and a uniter," and the Lord's Supper is the ultimate example of this.

Early leaders of the movement emphasized that the "union which Christ prayed might prevail among his disciples" is only possible through "love one for another". Without this love, the church is merely a social club rather than the "body of Christ". The Lord's Supper is a time to "strive for peace" and to "honor one another above yourselves". It is a tangible reminder that "we who are many are one, for we all partake of the one loaf".

The Lord's Supper is a "tangible reminder of all we share," and it serves to "enrich our lives, our care for one another, and our conversations". It is the "golden chain" of love that binds the "repentant sinners to Christ and other believers".

Proclaiming the Lord's Death: The Message to the World

The Lord's Supper is not only an internal ritual for the church but a powerful "proclamation" to the world. The Apostle Paul wrote, "For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes". This "proclamation" (kataggellein) is an "address to people," a "word of God" that is spoken through the act of the meal. It identifies the "crucified Christ" as the central message of the church, meeting the observer as both a "promise" of salvation and a "warning" of the consequences of sin.

The importance of this proclamation lies in its focus on the "sacrificial character" of Christ's death. The bread and wine are "symbols" and "reminders" that "Jesus died" and that His death was a "sacrifice for us". By participating in the supper, the church is "sharing in His death" and receiving the "benefits" of it—righteousness, peace, and the removal of sins. It is a testimony that the "redemption of his people was purchased upon condition of humility and continual obedience".

The proclamation is also a "testimony of Christ's return". When we "proclaim the Lord's death until He comes," we are looking forward to the "glorious inheritance waiting for us in the next life". The Lord's Supper is a "memorial of His great sacrifice," but it is also a "pointer to that wonderful day when we will hold Communion in heaven with our resurrected Lord". It keeps the "blessed hope" of the Second Coming alive in the hearts of the believers.

Early members of the faith understood this proclamation as part of the "third angel's message"—the final warning and invitation to the world. They saw themselves as "watchmen" on the "watch-tower," sounding the cry that "the morning cometh, and also the night". The Lord's Supper is the "refresher" for the "army of God" as they prepare for the "last battle". It is the "primitive faith" that "searched them out" and prepared them for "translation at the coming of the Lord".

By celebrating the Lord's Supper, the church "testifies to the truth of our unity in Jesus" and "rebels against the ways of the world". It is a "living faith" that is "active and obedient," purifying the believer "even as he is pure".

Pioneer History and the "Quarterly Meeting" Tradition

In the early history of the Seventh-day Adventist church, the Lord's Supper was not celebrated every week, but typically became a part of what was called the "quarterly meeting". Because the members were often "widely scattered" across rural areas, they would meet together once every three months for a period of intensive spiritual focus. This practice helped to preserve the "solemnity and meaning" of the occasion, preventing the ordinance from becoming a routine or casual event.

The quarterly meeting was a multi-faceted event. It began with a "preparatory service" where the "membership roll was read," and "each member gave a testimony regarding his Christian experience". This was a time for "self-examination" and "the reconciling of differences". Only after this thorough spiritual preparation, and "if an ordained minister or elder was present," was the Lord's Supper celebrated. The "ordinance of washing feet" was always connected with the supper, serving as the final preparation for the communion table.

Prominent leaders like Uriah Smith, who served as the editor of the "Review and Herald" for many years, were instrumental in codifying the "Synopsis of the Present Truth" and ensuring that the ordinances were understood and practiced with "dignity and poise". Smith and others were pained by "careless and undignified" administrations of the sacred rite and worked to provide "books instructing us how to administer the rite" correctly. They believed that the Lord's Supper was "second in importance and significance only to the rite of baptism".

James White also contributed significantly to the understanding of the "Present Truth" regarding the ordinances. He emphasized that the "redemption of his people" was the central theme of the Gospel and that the Lord's Supper was the primary way to "keep fresh in their remembrance" the "solemn scenes of His betrayal and crucifixion". He argued that the "salvation of men depends upon a continual application" of Christ's blood, and the supper was the "appointed way" to facilitate this.

The historical development of these practices was not without struggle. The pioneers had to "investigate truth and compare results" amid "confusion and divisions" in the early Adventist groups. They sought to find a "safe place to plant their feet" on the "truths of the Bible" rather than on human impressions or traditions. Their commitment to the "plain commandment" of God led them to preserve the "ordinance of washing feet" even when it was being rejected by other denominations.

It continues to serve as a "refresher" for the soul, a "bath of renewed spiritual strength" that points forward to the "wonderful day when we will hold Communion in heaven". The history of these "spiritual forebears" is not negligible; it "heartens and incites to heavenly emulation" the believers of the present day.

Spiritual Presence: The Ministry of Unseen Messengers

A profound aspect of the Lord's Supper, as taught by Jesus and expanded upon by early writers, is the "unseen presence" of the Divine during the service. Ellen White states that "unseen messengers are present at the Lord's Supper," including "holy angels to strengthen us". This understanding elevates the ordinance from a mere mental exercise of memory to a "deeply personal experience" where the participant "hears His still small voice speaking to us" and "feels the Holy Spirit working on our hearts".

The presence of these heavenly beings serves several purposes. First, they "strengthen us" and help us to "feel deeply Christ's presence". Second, they shield the participants from "messengers from the prince of darkness" who try to "divert our attention" and "turn our thinking from the solemnity of the occasion". The Lord's Supper is a spiritual battlefield where the "clamor of error and heresy" is met with the "voice of truth". The "presence of holy angels" ensures that "Satan's angels can have no influence on us" during this sacred time.

This "mystical union" is also described using metaphors like the "vine and the branches" or the "golden chain". The Holy Spirit "makes Christ present within" the believer, providing "moral and spiritual power". This union is "formed by repentance and faith" and is "maintained by living, active faith". Engaging in the ordinance is a "spiritual discipline" that "strengthens a believer's union with Christ" through the "daily surrender of the will".

Furthermore, all Christians are "ordained unto the life of Christ" and are "God's messengers," whether or not they have had "ministerial hands" laid upon them. The Lord's Supper is a time when Christ Himself "lays His hands upon you" and says, "Ye are My witnesses". It is a "spiritual ordination" for ministry, where the believer is "dedicated to His sacred work" of "uplifting humanity". This "source of power" is the "Holy Spirit, through whom the Father and the Son pour forth the abundance of their vision and energy and grace".

The "unseen messengers" assist the believer in "parting with things below" and "placing affections on things above". It is a "refuge in prayer" and a "holy calm" that prepares the soul for the "bustling scenes of life" that follow.

The Eschatological Hope: Proclaiming Until He Comes

The Lord's Supper is fundamentally oriented toward the future. While it looks back to the cross, it also looks forward to the "Second Coming of Christ in power and glory". The command to observe the rite "until he comes" infuses every celebration with "hope and direction". It is a "testimony of Christ's return," a public declaration that "we have hope knowing that there is more than just this life".

Early believers saw the Lord's Supper as a "link to the marriage supper of the Lamb". They expected to "hold Communion in heaven" with their resurrected Lord.
